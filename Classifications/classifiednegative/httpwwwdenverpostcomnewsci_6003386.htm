Vets salute dogs left behind

Ex-handlers want memorial for canines that saved lives

By Claire Martin
Denver Post Staff Writer
Posted:   05/28/2007 01:00:00 AM MDT
Updated:   05/28/2007 09:06:20 AM MDT

Rick Claggett, shown with Dante, recalls dogs being taken away when handlers left Vietnam: "You build this bond that lasts forever, and ... your partner has no option at the end." (Post / RJ Sangosti)

During his tour in Vietnam, Rick Claggett and his dog scouted for enemy soldiers and booby traps, sleeping, eating and dodging bullets together.

"You build this bond that lasts forever, and you build it knowing that your partner has no option at the end," said Claggett, who lives in the southwest Denver area. "We get to go home, and the dogs don't."

His partner, a German Shepherd named Big Boy, was one of more than 4,000 military K-9 dogs trained to serve in the Vietnam War.

To commemorate Big Boy and other military dogs that have served alongside U.S. troops since World War II, Claggett and other members of the Vietnam Dog Handlers Association are lobbying Congress for a national war-dog memorial. Legislation approving a privately funded memorial is pending in Congress.

All but about 200 of the dogs that served in Vietnam died there. About 300 were killed in action - fatally wounded by enemy troops or victims of tropical diseases or infections. The rest were put down by military veterinarians or given to the South Vietnamese Army.

"The day we stood down in 1972, (Gen. William) West moreland was pinning medals on us as I watched the South Vietnamese take our dogs away," Claggett said. "Today, talking about this, I still choke up. The government should n't have let this happen."

Animals now adopted

In 2000, a law was enacted that allowed dogs too old for military service to be adopted instead of put down.

Claude "Butch" Stuart, another Denver member of the Vietnam Dog Handlers Association, said the dogs were key to winning battles.

"The dogs were truly a weapon system that saved American lives and also caused a lot of bad guys to lose their lives," he said.

His partner in Vietnam was named King. Nearly 37 years later, Stuart can recite from memory King's military ID number - 1A90.

Stuart, Claggett and the other dog handlers fiercely loved their dogs, treating them as equals. Stuart remembers a story about a dog handler whispering to his dog, Scratch, as the two stood sentry after a day of fighting North Vietnamese troops in the surrounding hillsides.

A fellow Marine was dumbfounded to hear the handler was giving Scratch the same briefing their unit heard earlier that evening. About 10 minutes later, he heard Scratch barking moments before grenades exploded downhill. They fought a battalion of North Vietnamese soldiers that night, killing or wounding most of them. Scratch had foiled an ambush.

"The units fortunate enough to have dogs working with them considered the dogs gold - better than gold," Stuart said.

Following his dog, a handler watched closely, knowing the nuances of a pricked ear or a suddenly tensed muscle meant the difference between life and death.

Big Boy sat at attention when his sensitive ears caught the subtle whistle of tripwire slicing the breeze or smelled explosives in a booby trap. Stuart's dog, King, had a way of twitching his ears when he sensed enemy soldiers.

When a handler's tour of duty ended, sometimes there was time to acclimate his dog with a new handler. More often, military policy dictated the dog's death.

But before the law passed in 2000 allowed adoptions, exceptions rarely were granted despite a handler's pleas.

"King was a four-legged Marine to me," Stuart said. "The morning I left, I cried like a baby. I was leaving my partner, my buddy, my pal, my protector."
