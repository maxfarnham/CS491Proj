    Big error in this report: U.S. banks will only issue "chip-and-signature" cards; none are issuing true "chip-and-PIN", for some dumb reason. Chip-and-signature cards generally won't work at automatic kiosks, such as those at non-U.S. train stations selling tickets. Also, merchants in some European countries might only accept them reluctantly, because, in the case of fraud, the merchant bears the liability for accepting a chip-and-signature card transaction from a customer, whereas, for chip-and-PIN, the bank bears the liability.

                  Lampshade Wile E. Coli • 9 hours ago

                  The cards that I have seen for this are not RFID cards, but smartcards with contact patches. Also, I have yet to see if any bank is actually issuing chip+PIN (as should be done). The big difference between the RFID cars you mention and those with an actual chip is the ability to perform interactive transactions. When a one-time transaction ID can be sent to and signed by a process running on a chip (rather than a one-way data dump from storage), there is no way to "skim" the card for future replication.
