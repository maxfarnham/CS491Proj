                    I appreciate the reply.

                    You are absolutely correct that we should question things. But that also leads to a question: when do we attain a level of satisfaction with an explanation to the extent that we can move beyond the questions it answers? For instance, you may find this a ridiculous example, but I think it drives home the point I want to make. My example is: is it OK, today, to question the atomic nature of matter? The answer is, of course, yes. But no working scientist today should be subject to criticism for accepting, without question, that atoms exist. At this stage, the atomic nature of matter is well-established: it is settled.

                    Now I know that most global warming skeptical folk don't like to hear that word as it conjures up images of scientists stifling inquiry. But to me that is not what settled means. To me it means that there exist results of climate science that rest on well-established science. To question these climate science results would mean to also question the results of other fields that also rest on the same well-established science.

                    For example, I was having a debate with another commenter recently about the correctness of the greenhouse gas theory. The ideas of the greenhouse gas theory rest on well-established results of physics, such as why and how molecules absorb and radiate IR radiation. If one is going to undermine the greenhouse gas theory, you're going to have to undermine some very basic quantum mechanics. One indeed might be successful at that, but then again...

                    All of this is a very long winded way to finally come around to your multiple questions about the "adjustments" to the temperature record. Now I'll be the first to say that these adjustments fall into a different category from those of quantum mechanics. But, questions surrounding the adjustments have been asked and answered time and again. At this point I don't fault climate scientists who are knowledgeable about these adjustments to consider the point settled.

                    Does that mean that someone won't find an error, or figure out a superior way to handle the issues that surround the raw temperature data? No, of course not. But I do think that enough analysis of these adjustments have been made, so that working climate scientists can confidently use the temperature records in other research.

                    A very good overview of the adjustments and their impacts to temperature records can be found here:

                    http://judithcurry.com/2015/02...

                    In case you need some background info, the authors of this piece are associated with a group called Berkeley Earth. They too had serious scientific questions about the temperature record so they formed their own group and did an entirely independent analysis, using different approaches to adjustments than those used by others, such as the NOAA. I'll let the essay speak for itself.

                    If this weren't so long already I'd address your other questions too.

                                                Yes, and…??

                                                Oh well, I have to assume you are trying to use the linked article to claim that we still don’t know precisely the total yearly amount of CO2 exhausted by volcanoes. Probably true. But that still doesn’t make Huebsch’s
                                                statement, which concerns single volcanic eruptions, correct – we know enough to analyze and falsify that.

                                                But let’s dig into the LiveScience post a little deeper. The author cites a 2013 paper by Mike Burton as giving the most recent estimates. The link is broken in the post, but it is fairly easy to find the paper she is referring to. It is here (you'll have to copy and past into your browser):

                                                file:///C:/Users/gslough/Downloads/2013_Burton%20et%20al_book_chapter.pdf

                                                Now the 2011 Gerlach paper that the Wired author links to in his blog post (found here: http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com... ), gives a range for volcanic CO2 emissions of 0.13 to 0.44 gigatons, although they use as a working range 0.14 to 0.26 gigatons per year. The Burton paper gives an estimate of 0.54 gigatons per year – about twice the working number used by Gerlach, but not a big enough change to be meaningful. In fact, Burton et al. state:

                                                The global subaerial CO2 flux we report is higher than previous estimates, but remains insignificant relative to anthropogenic emissions, which are two orders of magnitude greater at 35,000 Mt/yr [35 gigatons per year] (Friedlingstein et al. 2010). Nevertheless, it is clear that uncertainties in volcanic CO2 emission rates remain high and significant upward revisions of the lithospheric CO2 flux cannot be ruled out.

                                                So the take away is that volcanic CO2 emissions are still uncertain, but do not, and are not expected to, form a significant fraction of anthropogenic emissions.
