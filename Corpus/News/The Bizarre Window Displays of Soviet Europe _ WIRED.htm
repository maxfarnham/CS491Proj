The Bizarre Window Displays of Soviet Europe

Launch Slideshow
Loading

Walking around cities like Prague and Krakow in the late 1980s, American David Hlynsky was struck by the lack of advertising on the streets. Instead of Pepsi and the Marlboro man, shop windows displayed scant offerings of everyday items like bread or plumbing supplies. The lack of frivolity fascinated him.

“In the dying days of the Cold War, I saw these windows as a vast ad hoc museum of a great failing utopia,” Hlynsky writes.

He documented the crumbling aesthetic in 450 windows across the Soviet Union and the Eastern Bloc. A portion of his work recently was published in Window-Shopping Through the Iron Curtain. His photos capture a world on the brink of collapse, one in which a strange blend of Communism and consumerism converge.

Hlynsky highlights the quiet but telling nature of life in Poland, Yugoslavia, the USSR and other communist countries. The displays appear worn and faded, all muted colors of ocher and rust red. Cardboard cutouts and threadbare fabrics dress them up. Even the windows and facades were poorly kept, with cracking varnish, chipped stone work, and peeling paint. If the shopkeepers did try to fancy anything up, they didn’t get far.

“If you could imagine that the art director [at the store] was your kindergarten teacher, and the advertising was accomplished with blunt-nose scissors and craft paper, that’s what it was like,” he says.

Before visiting the Eastern Bloc, Hlynsky had a narrow view of communist countries. Growing up in the McCarthy Era, the photographer was told everyone under communist rule was oppressed and backward. Though the windows he captured often were basic compared to the West, Hlynsky found the cities and the people to be as sophisticated and interesting as any Westerner. “I realized that not everyone [in the Eastern Bloc] raised pigs and that their cities could be just elegant as Paris,” he says.

Hlynsky came to appreciate the simplicity of the shops and the lifestyle they encouraged. Instead of worrying about what brand to buy, people in the Eastern Bloc shopped for what they needed and moved on. Although he certainly doesn’t advocate for communism, Hlynsky appreciates the uncomplicated idea of less. “I actually started to be relieved when I was there,” he says. “I felt like I could walk down the street without having to accommodate everyone’s sales pitch.”
