    It is not merely pedantic to point out that encryption products are not privacy enhancing technologies; they are secrecy technologies.

    Privacy is different from secrecy. Privacy is about restraint; it is about limiting the personal data that is collected, limiting what is done with data once collected, and being open with people about what data you have about them. You can have privacy without secrecy.

    Indeed, privacy is the protection you need precisely when your data is not secret. And the users of products like Anonabox had best remember this because sooner or later, their encryption is going to break down, and then what are they going to do? Secrecy is brittle - it cannot be relied upon. We need privacy regulations rather than futile, do-it-yourself pseudo privacy tech.

            Steve_Lockstep Adrian Wade 5 days ago

            Thanks Adrian.
            My take is that privacy in general is less about what is done with personal information than what is not done with it. Therefore lasting privacy solutions have to be more political than technological.
            My particular take in the case of Anonabox is it kinda proves the general point. Even when encryption works as intended, it locks users into circles of secrecy all using the same technology. Encryption, for the foreseeable future, is incompatible with open e-commerce and life on line in general. But encryption hardly ever works as intended. It's terribly brittle, so even if you make secrecy your prime privacy strategy, you have to plan for the scenario of encryption failing. You still need regulatory safeguards to protect personal data when it falls into the wrong hands.
            A more robust privacy paradigm than everyone-for-themselves PETs is Data Protection regulations. In 100-odd countries, DP laws protect personal data against misuse even when it comes from the public domain. Remember the Google StreetView Wi-Fi collection episode? That was resolved in favour of the plaintiffs within weeks in Australia, Korea, Japan, Holland and elsewhere, because the DP laws there restrict the use of personal data even if sniffed from unencrypted "public" transmissions (I note that in the US, after three years the case is still in the courts, because there is no broad based privacy law). The point being that even when you lose control of your data, you can still expect privacy. Privacy is what you need when your affairs are not anonymous.
            Of course, Data Protection laws are not perfect, but neither is any encryption product.

                    Steve_Lockstep Adrian Wade 5 days ago

                    I pretty well agree with all that. I spoke very loosely when I said 'encryption was incompatible with e-commerce'. Of course SSL etc is essential and works well. What I had in mind was the ad hoc person-to-person "PETs". And I mean "e-commerce" as it exists today. We might re-imagine e-commerce in all sorts of ways, but experience shows that strong point-to-point encryption (eg secure email) is still utopian. I wish it were otherwise.
                    I have to say I have mixed feelings about my doctor encrypting records. On its face of course it's a good idea, but they need a high degree of expertise to manage it properly. It's astonishing how many small doctors' practices collapse from malware, or fall prey to ransomware (when a criminal encrypts the doctor's file servers and insists on a ransom to free the data -- why the hell can't these victims rebuild their system?). The sad moral is that small businesses are hopeless at security (for which I blame the technology not the businesses) so I just can't get too excited about encryption in real life in these environments. It tends to fail un-safe - either with the exposure of sensitive data, or the total loss of data.
