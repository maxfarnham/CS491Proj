Game of Thrones Recap: This Season, Everything Changes

Spoilers for the Season 5 premiere of Game of Thrones follow, obviously.

Game of Thrones is finally back, and all bets are off.

We’ve reached a very interesting point in the Game of Thrones television series: Not only will the show start to dramatically depart from the plot of the books, but thanks to their slow publication pace, will soon catch up to them, and even move beyond them. Will these new storylines be spoilers for the eventual books, inventions by the showrunners, or some combination? We may not always know, so there could be plenty of surprises in store for even the most devoted readers.

Each week, I’ll be recapping Game of Thrones and dissecting the changes between the show and the books, and I’ll do my best not to spoil any important plot points. But again, we’re entering thorny and uncharted territory here, where we won’t always know what a spoiler really is—or what the “real” story is. Get ready.

Cersei

We open on two young, noble ladies, wandering the woods in beautiful dresses. One seems afraid, but the other—an intense, blond-haired young girl with a massive sense of entitlement—fearlessly insists that they keep walking towards an ominous hut. SURPRISE! It’s Li’l Cersei. She drags her friend into the hut to meet a witch, and after the usual Lannister threats of mutilation and such, the witch agrees to answer three questions about Cersei’s future.

She’s been promised to the prince—that would have been Prince Rhaegar Targaryen—but the witch reveals Cersei won’t marry the prince after all, but rather the king (aka Robert Baratheon). The witch also reveals Cersei will be queen for a time until another younger, more beautiful queen comes along “to cast you down and take all you hold dear.” When Cersei asks about their potential children, she gets the real kicker: “The king will have 20 children, and you will have three. Gold will be their crowns, and gold their shrouds.” The 20 children are the many bastards of Robert Baratheon, of course, while her three children are Joffrey, Myrcella, and Tommen, who have received precisely two gold crowns and one golden shroud so far.

Back in the present, Cersei is on her way to another funeral: the final rites of Tywin Lannister. She glances at Margaery as she ascends the steps to the Great Sept—thinking, no doubt, of the younger, more beautiful queen. Could those prophecies be the reason that Cersei has always been so angry, so paranoid? Has she lived her entire life in a defensive posture since that one fateful afternoon in the woods? She goes to meet Jaime who has been standing guard by their father’s body; he tells her that they’ve finally inherited everything Tywin built for them, and they need to stay united now. Cersei’s pretty laser-focused on killing Tyrion now, though, and blames Jaime for releasing him. “Tyrion may be a monster,” she says, “but at least he killed our father on purpose.”

After the funeral, we see the return of Lancel, the Lannister cousin that Cersei hooked up with when Jaime was gone. He’s transformed from a waifish boy into a broad-shouldered young man in a crewcut who walks around barefoot in a simple robe. His father, Kevan, explains that he’s joined the Sparrows, a cult of religious fanatics who worship the Seven. (Imagine the Christian trinity, but with seven incarnations, instead of three.) He hilariously asks Cersei’s forgiveness for leading her into temptation—”I doubt you lead anyone anywhere,” snarks Cersei—as well as for what happened with the King. It seems he was the one who spiked Robert’s wine during that fateful, fatal boar hunt. He doesn’t bring it up as a threat, exactly, but that doesn’t mean it couldn’t someday be one.

In the books: The witch has a name: Maggy the Frog. Cersei finds her in Lannisport, the city where she grew up, along with her friends Melara Heatherspoon and Jeyne Farman. Jeyne ran away before the prophecies, but Melara got a prophecy of her own: that she would die young. It is later implied that Cersei killed her by pushing her down a well. The prophecy also got more specific about Cersei’s downfall, suggesting that she would be strangled to death by a “valonqar.” It’s a Valyrian word with some potentially big implications—if we assume the prophecy will come true—so Google it at your own risk.

Jaime and Cersei meet up at the scene of Tywin’s murder, rather than the sept, although their conversation is equally unpleasant. The first time we hear about the Sparrows is when Brienne encounters them taking the bones of septons murdered in the War of Five Kings to the Sept of Baelor, to seek protection from the King. Lancel does become a religious zealot involved with the Sparrows, but instead of getting sexier, we’re told he looks older and thinner.

Tyrion

It’s been a rough few weeks for Tyrion. He was wrongly accused of murdering his nephew, sentenced to death, ended up murdering both his lover and his father, and eventually sealed himself in a crate for a voyage across the Narrow Sea to the city of Pentos. Varys sure does like solving problems by putting people in boxes. When Varys releases Tyrion, he finds himself at the home of Illyrio Mopatis, the man who brokered Daenerys’s marriage to Khal Drogo—and gave her the dragon eggs—as well as the man Arya overheard scheming with Varys way back in Season 1.

Turns out Varys and Illyrio are part of a shadowy cabal that thought Robert Baratheon sucked and wanted to restore the Targaryens for the good of the realm. But thanks to a long series of bad decisions—aka the whole plot of the show—things have only gotten worse in the Seven Kingdoms. Tyrion’s still got the murdered-a-bunch-of-people-I-loved blues, so he’s mostly just interested in drinking a lot of wine and talking about how everything’s shitty. But Varys wants Tyrion to get himself together and help put someone on the Iron Throne who has both the lineage and temperament to rule well. “Good luck finding him,” sniffs Tyrion. “Who said anything about ‘him’?” replies Varys. And so it looks like they’re off to Meereen, to meet Daenerys.

In the books: Tyrion is shipped across the sea in a cask with the help of Varys, and while the Spider disappears from King’s Landing, he does not accompany Tyrion on the trip. Instead, Illyrio is the one who frees Tyrion upon his arrival, and tells him about the glorious plans for a Targaryen restoration.

Daenerys

From atop the Great Pyramid, the harpy falls. The enormous bronze figure is torn off the great structure as a symbolic gesture, but down in the streets of Meereen, the harpy is rising. A young Unsullied warrior—note, not Grey Worm—heads to the red light district of Meereen, where a sex worker asks him if he wants the “same as always.” She takes her clothes off and they get in bed to … just snuggle. It’s pretty adorable, at least until a man wearing an angry, golden mask reaches in to cut his throat.

We learn that the murderer was working with the Sons of the Harpy, a group of nobles who oppose the rule of Daenerys. She is pissed as hell and wants the murdered man buried with full honors in the Temple of the Graces, and while that will likely infuriate her foes even further, she doesn’t seem to care. We see Missandei approach Grey Worm to ask why an Unsullied soldier would visit a brothel—perhaps wondering if their smoldering sexual tension might have some possible resolution—but he replies that he doesn’t know the reason.

The Meereenese noble Hizdahr zo Loraq returns from his ambassadorial trip to Yunkai, the city Daenerys conquered earlier. They seem to have accepted the end of slavery, and created a new council of elders made up of both former slave-owners and former slaves. But now they have a request: They want to reopen the fighting pits as a place for optional life-or-death battles between free men, a tradition that even the pit fighters themselves supposedly support. Daenerys turns it down flat.

Later, during their candlelit sexy time, Daario asks her to reconsider. He reveals his own tragic backstory: He was sold into slavery at age 12, and trained for the fighting pits. But rather than hating them, he believes they saved him. He also advises her that now is the time to show power in other ways, not with the Unsullied but with the other aces up her sleeve: “You’re not the mother of Unsullied. You’re the mother of dragons.”

She decides to visit the dragon pit where Viserion and Rhaegal were chained after the death of the little girl. Much like Tyrion, they’re pretty pissed off about being crated for so long, and they greet her by lunging and breathing flames into the air. Daenerys runs from the room in a panic, but it’s hard to tell whether she’s actually scared of the dragons, or what she’s done to them.

In the books: More than one Unsullied soldier is killed by the Sons of the Harpy, including one who died at a brothel, though it took six men to take him down. Although there is no sexual tension between Missandei and Grey Worm in the books—mostly because she’s 10 years old—when Daenerys asks him why the Unsullied would visit prostitutes, he has a much better response: “Even those who lack a man’s parts may still have a man’s heart.”

Sansa

Now that Petyr Baelish is the sole guardian of Robin Arryn, the little Lord of the Eyrie, he’s trying to get the coddled boy toughened up. He’s finally getting some combat training, and Littlefinger has decided to leave him with Lord Royce, one of the loyal lords of the Vale, as he and Sansa head out on a journey. Littlefinger tells Royce that they’re heading for the Fingers, a coastal area of peninsulas to the east (and the origin of Littlefinger’s name, as his family rules the smallest of the Fingers). But Sansa quickly realizes this was misdirection, and that they’re heading west instead. “Where are we going?” she asks. “To a land so far from here that even Cersei Lannister can’t get her hands on you,” he replies.

In the books: From what we’ve seen so far in the books, Sansa doesn’t leave the Eyrie; Littlefinger’s machinations require that she stay put in the Vale. Now that her “father” is the Lord Protector, Sansa becomes the Lady of the Vale in all but name, looking after Robin and learning more about the art of intrigue as Littlefinger moves his pieces about the chessboard.

Brienne

After defeating the Hound but losing Arya, Brienne is having a crisis of confidence, lashing out at her poor squire, Pod. She tells him that she doesn’t want him following her anymore, because she’s not a leader. “All I ever wanted was to fight for a lord I believed in,” she laments. “The good lords are dead, and the rest of them monsters.” Pod seems relatively unfazed by her tantrum, and encourages her to focus on finding Sansa. And who rolls by them in a carriage at that very moment, but Sansa and Littlefinger! Is this going to be another one of those near misses, or could the fulfillment of her vow to Catelyn finally be at hand?

In the books: Brienne’s quest in the books involves a lot of going around in circles and fighting randos. In videogame terms, all she really does is grind. She never fought the Hound, she never encountered Arya, and she never crossed paths with Sansa, either directly or indirectly. Needless to say, the TV version has been a lot more fun. I have no idea where her story is going now, but I’m excited to find out.

Jon

King Stannis, who has taken up residence at the Wall with his army, asks Jon to take a proposal for the Wildlings: Join his army, fight the Lannisters, and when he wins he’ll pardon them all, make them citizens, and give them land. It’s a very fair offer, but one that Jon knows the Free Folk—whose entire deal is about not kneeling for anyone—are unlikely to jump at. Stannis gives Jon till nightfall to convince Mance Rayder to bend the knee, or he burns.

Mance is understandably upset about the idea of being burned alive, but he still turns the offer down. Plus, he’s not even sure he could even convince the Free Folk to follow Stannis if he wanted to. “They followed me because they respected me,” he says. “The moment I kneel for a southern king, that’s all gone.” And so that night, Mance gets dragged out to a pyre and given one last chance to “kneel and live.” He wishes Stannis good fortune instead, and after yet another crazy speech from Melisandre about light and darkness, the burning begins. We watch Mance as the fear and panic and agony rise, and just as it starts to get truly awful—THUMP. An arrow in his chest, and Jon holding the bow.

In the books: We seem to be skipping over a few major story points, but Mance does burn—though he ends up screaming for mercy, and is not granted it. Not everything is exactly what it seems, however, although the show might simply be skipping over those complications. Also, Jon has archers from the Watch put Mance out of his misery, rather than doing it himself.
