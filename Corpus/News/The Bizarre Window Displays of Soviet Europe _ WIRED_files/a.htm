                                Frederick Murre 4 days ago

                                Our US family once lodged some touring Russian folk musicians during the Soviet era. They were pretty good sorts. The musicians, who like many Russians of the period were fairly handy craftsmen - were also reduced to tears when my father took them down to the local discount store and bought them several packs of jig saw blades. The tears weren't so much at my father's kindness, but stress, knowing that US $4.95 would buy two dozen + jigsaw blades of good quality - and was for us, pocket change. The cost of a meal. Supply wasn't a problem either. This was a small southern backwoods town - no Potempkin village.

                                Basic staples of life were more or less available after the 50s in the USSR, I mean you could meet your caloric needs. But for craftspeople - even basic tools were just not 'available' Unless lifted and bribed from worksites or places of production. And they were needed. The average craftsman and handyman in the USSR basically had to scrape everything together just to have enough resources to undertake repairs of everything domestic and then some. Everything that wasn't military or important enough to have a state committee running it- those things were pretty much just held together with bailing wire and odd sized nails for the duration.

                                                                                                            DH 3 days ago

                                                                                                            The article in WIRED does not include the three essays in the book. My own essay described the ingenuity of an underground economy operating behind these windows. I should also say that I met many smart, cultured, ironic, intelligent and creative people while traveling there and, as in the West, their happiness didn't hinge on whether they could buy ostentatious luxury goods or not. Family and community can and do thrive both within and outside of their opportunity to consume more than their essential needs. Even our most tired cliche claims that "money can't buy you love."

                                                                                                            What I did discover is that I myself had lived my entire life surrounded by the artificially happy faces of advertising models. We ought not be tricked by our own shop windows. These photoshopped gods and goddesses reflect an effortless prosperity that can neither be achieved nor sustained. Our advertising machine drives our consumption to the point of keeping us all (and our environment) in debt. Our advertising culture gives us permission to dismiss the needs of the poor, mock the homely, fill the oceans with plastic packaging and spend like there's no tomorrow.

                                                                                                            I would never promote the communist model as a desirable alternative. It was clearly not. But, as I have written, those systems were overthrown through intellectual discourse from within and not by cowboy brinksmanship. History cannot be reduced to the simplicity of a spy thriller. Real people live real lives in real time.

                                                                                                            The failure of communism does not automatically give us a free ticket to limitless growth. I fear that the jingoism of some responders can too easily render us as spotless champions of the world. We are not.

                                                                                                            While defending free enterprise, we often turn a blind eye to our own massive release of environmental pollutants. We participate as slave owners in the guise of stock holders in third world sweat shops. Our malls are filled to the brim with goods too cheap to warrant our own precious labour. We hypocritically support oil and resource wars while claiming to support citizens' freedom. Shock and Awe was aimed at taking down one renegade despot but it actually killed countless innocents and displaced millions. Our profit/loss calculations have given us the logic to support all sorts of tyranny.

                                                                                                            Don't let these empty shop windows vindicate the glut of our own mega malls. It is the old paradigm. It is the propaganda we absorb as truth. That is exactly my point. Let's examine these windows and the effect they have had on our own assumed entitlements. Could the free market blind us with bling?

                                                                                                            Civil rights, gay rights, feminism, labour unions, pacifism, environmentalism, public education and universal health care have all been derided as "communist plots" at one time or another.

                                                                                                            Yes we are free but are we also responsible?
