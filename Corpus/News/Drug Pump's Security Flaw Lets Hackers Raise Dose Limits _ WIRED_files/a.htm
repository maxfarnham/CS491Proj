            caribis2 • 5 days ago

            Some of the decisions made by Hospira are inexcusable. Hard coding a backdoor password and not encrypting user ids or passwords was not acceptable in 2000 when the software was probably written. Choosing to push out updates, in an hospital environment, is an understandable decision. Deciding not to place extra security around it in 2000 - yeah, okay.

            The point to be made is that the first pump was released in 2002. With medical device program development times, the project was probably started 1998/1999. This highlights the difference between an iPhone and industrial equipment: life cycles. Industrial equipment, including hospital equipment, takes longer to develop and release and lives on in use for decades. A phone with a camera was cool in 2002. When you're designing a new medical device today you have to try and plan your device architecture for a 20 year horizon. Hacking tools and other threats that won't be developed until 2020 have to be considered today. How you manage that is the real question.

            Also, you have to factor in that these companies aren't Apple, Facebook or Google. They don't have thousands of engineers on a model line. The entire team may be a dozen engineers (including software, electrical & mechanical) responsible for the new product and a half dozen legacy products. The resources don't exist to update legacy models, add new features to the existing line and introduce the new model.

                      LoudRambler • 5 days ago

                      This whole ordeal is just another example of gotcha journalism, designed to make readers think "ZOMG they can hack pumps".

                      Well, yes, they can. If they manage to hack into a hospital system, and, on top of this, somehow create a spoofed drug library. Which, at worst, will make pumps not override bad numbers manually punched in.

                      I'd say that this is a typical example of deliberate design where a non mission-critical part is made less secure to make for easier releases and, in process, for less vulnerability in the overall system by isolating it to begin with. It's like hacking some video advertizing monitor: yes, you can broadcast pron there, like it happened in Moscow about two years ago, but so what? It's easy to pull the plug and issue apologies, as opposed to creating some heavy logic around non-critical issue that would allow to hack servers by pretending to be a pump.

                      This is similar to "hacking" cars by opening up the controller board and connecting wires to an input soldiered to it: yes, it is possible, but is it practical?
