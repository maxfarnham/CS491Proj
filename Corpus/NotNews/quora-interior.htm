    I did an interesting thing in the middle of my teaching career that I maintained until the end. I accepted late work but marked each late piece with a very large inked "L" at the top. When queried by students as to what it meant, I was always intentionally ambiguous in my explanation:

    "The "L" means that the paper was late."
    "So, what does that mean?" they would ask, expecting some number.
    "It means that you handed it in after the announced deadline."

    Here is the weird thing. Students would rush to my office after class to hand in something they might have left in their dorm room and they would invariably ask: "Do I still get an "L" ? Eventually, the hatred of the "L" trumped any of the point docking I had done earlier in my career. 

    One of my colleagues, who heard about the hated "L" from her advisee adopted the practice and had the same result. It reduced the amount of late work and created a stigma about handing things in late.

    Grades are based on whatever the teacher says they're based on. If the teacher says your grade is based on not just how well you did the work, but whether or not you turned it in by the deadline specified, then that's what the grade is based on. Follow directions. That's part of the lesson you're supposed to learn -- not just the subject matter. The teacher directed you to turn in your work at a specific date. You didn't follow those directions, so you got penalized.

    I'm going to be honest here: I felt the same way you did in school, and I made the exact same argument. Do you know why? Because I was so damn sure I was smarter than everyone else. I suspect you feel similarly, but here's the thing: you probably aren't as smart as you think you are, and even if you are, that matters less than you realize.

    It might surprise you to learn that in the real world, following directions is pretty important. It's not just about what you know or what you do, and it's not about whether you did the things that you think are important. It's about whether you followed the directions of the person in charge. You might as well learn this simple fact now and get used to doing things -- even if you don't think they're necessary, and you might as well get used to doing things on someone else's timeline and in the manner he or she chooses. Because a lot of real life works that way.

    Yes. I actually give an F for late submissions.

    When I give an assignment, I give it to the whole class and everyone has the same deadline. I make it clear that late submissions won't be read or graded and those conditions are made clear at the start of the course and repeated often.

    If I receive too many requests for clarifications before the deadline, I post the answers on the course blog for all students to see. As a teacher, my job is as much to help my students understand the concepts in a subject as it is for me to help you get ready for life, by understanding the practical aspects of concepts such as respect, punctuality and fairness.

    A student who turns in the work on time has demonstrated that he or she can manage time (punctuality), appreciate that the peer group has the same constraints (fairness), and also expect the same treatment as others (respect for others' time). All good life skills.

    I give individual feedback so that even where there is no right answer, the student understands where they could have improved their understanding and their communication of their understanding of the concepts being tested. These are two different skills to learn, that help get ready for life.

    I do not even read late submissions because it wastes everyone's time. If you don't understand things, ask me. If you need an extension, ask me. If you can't turn in your work on time even when these options are available, you don't get graded and earn an F. The behaviour is disrespectful of your peers and the teacher. It comes with a penalty. It is simple.

    I have written without using profanity because I think the vocabulary we share is sufficient for us to communicate clearly. And if you think the schooling process isn't about life skills as much as it is about learning concepts then I think you have very low expectations of school and education, and possibly your teachers.

    As a young teen, I would regularly do homework assignments badly in subjects I understood well.  I have one end of term report that marked me as last in the class on homework, and first in the class in the final exam (I got 100%).

    Even at that extreme, I regret not doing my homework with more care.  I learnt some poor study habits, and when my subjects got hard (at university level) I wasn't prepared for it.   The poor discipline also leaked into subjects that I was less good at.

    I won a place at Oxford University at 16.  I gained exemplary exam results at school.  I failed to live up to my academic potential partly through ill-discipline learnt early.

    If your teacher truly believes that grades should be based only on knowledge, then no, they should not lower your grade for not turning in homework. They should also not lower your grade for skipping class, flipping over desks, or taking off your pants in the middle of their lecture.

    As you might be able to tell from my examples, most teachers do not believe this, and they grade accordingly.

    I suggest talking directly to your teacher, as none of us have insight into your particular situation. If you ask your teacher about their grading philosophy and listen to them with an open mind, then respectfully present your concerns, maybe you can come to understand each other's position better. Don't go in looking for an argument, as I doubt that will be productive, but often listening to one another can improve the relationship and resolve some of the issues you are having. Even if the policy doesn't change (which it likely won't), you might feel better about the whole situation afterward.

    [NOTE: I actually do believe that grades should be based on knowledge. Implementing a knowledge-based grading system as opposed to the usual compliance-based system is quite challenging and it's likely your teacher isn't interested in doing so. When I did implement such a system, the #1 comment I got in my student evaluations was: "Homework should be part of the grade," so I modified the system to include homework in subsequent years (to the great disappointment of students like yourself). Remember your teacher is responsible for more students than just you, what you want may not be what the majority of the class wants, and your teacher is responsible for teaching all of you in as fair a way as they can manage.]

    There are issues of fairness that I would ask you to consider.
     
    Student A can't find the answers to two questions on his homework; he hands in the assignment on time minus those two answers.
     
    Student B can't find two answers on his homework assignment. He spends another two days looking and finds the correct answers.
     
    Should student B receive a higher grade than student A?
     
    and
     
    The teacher goes over the entire homework assignment in class the day the work is collected. Is it fair for students who didn't do the homework to get the answers in class and then hand in the homework the next day, expecting an A?
